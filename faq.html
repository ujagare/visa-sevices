How long does the visa process take?

Visa processing times vary significantly depending on:

  • The type of visa you're applying for
  • The destination country
  • The current workload at the embassy or consulate
  • Seasonal factors (peak travel seasons often have longer processing times)
  • The completeness and accuracy of your application

As a general guideline:

  • Tourist visas: 2-4 weeks
  • Student visas: 4-12 weeks
  • Work visas: 4-16 weeks
  • Migration/PR visas: 6-24 months

We always recommend applying well in advance of your planned travel date. During your consultation, we'll provide you with current processing time estimates for your specific visa category.

Tourist Visas

How far in advance should I apply for a tourist visa?

We recommend applying for a tourist visa 2-3 months before your planned travel date. This provides sufficient time for:

  • Document preparation and verification
  • Application submission
  • Embassy processing (which can vary seasonally)
  • Addressing any additional requests from the embassy
  • Making travel arrangements once the visa is approved

For peak travel seasons (summer holidays, December-January period), consider applying even earlier as processing times may be longer due to high application volumes.

What financial documents do I need for a tourist visa?

Financial documentation is crucial for tourist visa applications as it demonstrates your ability to support yourself during your trip. Typically required documents include:

  • Bank statements for the last 6 months
  • Income tax returns for the last 2-3 years
  • Salary slips or employment letter stating your income (if employed)
  • Property ownership documents (if applicable)
  • Fixed deposits or investments (if applicable)
  • Sponsorship letter and sponsor's financial documents (if someone else is sponsoring your trip)

The specific requirements and minimum funds required vary by destination country. We'll guide you on the exact financial documentation needed for your specific visa application.

Do I need travel insurance for a tourist visa?

Travel insurance requirements vary by destination:

  • Schengen Countries: Travel insurance with medical coverage of at least €30,000 is mandatory
  • USA: Not mandatory but highly recommended
  • UK: Not mandatory but recommended
  • Canada: Not mandatory but recommended
  • Australia: Not mandatory but recommended

Even when not mandatory, we strongly recommend obtaining comprehensive travel insurance that covers:

  • Medical emergencies and hospitalization
  • Trip cancellation or interruption
  • Lost luggage and personal belongings
  • Emergency evacuation

We can assist you in obtaining suitable travel insurance that meets the requirements of your destination country.

Can I extend my tourist visa while abroad?

The possibility of extending a tourist visa varies by country:

  • USA: You can apply for an extension by filing Form I-539 before your authorized stay expires
  • Schengen Countries: Extensions are generally only granted in exceptional circumstances (medical emergencies, force majeure)
  • UK: You can apply to extend your standard visitor visa if you have a good reason and meet the eligibility requirements
  • Australia: You can apply for a Visitor visa (subclass 600) while in Australia if your current visa allows
  • Canada: You can apply to extend your stay as a visitor before your current status expires

Important considerations:

  • Always apply for an extension before your current visa expires
  • You must provide valid reasons for the extension
  • You may need to demonstrate continued financial means
  • Some countries have maximum stay limitations regardless of extensions

We recommend consulting with us before your trip if you anticipate needing a longer stay, as it's often easier to apply for the appropriate duration initially.

Student Visas

What are the key requirements for a student visa?

Key requirements for student visas typically include:

  • Acceptance letter from an accredited educational institution
  • Proof of financial resources to cover tuition fees and living expenses
  • Academic transcripts and certificates from previous education
  • English language proficiency test results (IELTS, TOEFL, etc.)
  • Statement of purpose explaining your study plans and career goals
  • Health insurance coverage for the duration of your stay
  • Proof of accommodation arrangements
  • Intent to return to your home country after completing studies

Specific requirements vary by country. For example:

  • USA: Requires Form I-20 from the institution and SEVIS fee payment
  • Canada: Requires a study permit and may require a biometrics appointment
  • UK: Requires a Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS) from the institution
  • Australia: Requires Genuine Temporary Entrant (GTE) assessment

Our team provides comprehensive guidance on meeting all requirements for your chosen study destination.

Can I work while studying abroad?

Work rights for international students vary by country:

  • USA: F-1 visa holders can work on-campus up to 20 hours per week during the academic year and full-time during breaks. Off-campus work is generally only permitted through Optional Practical Training (OPT) or Curricular Practical Training (CPT).
  • Canada: Most study permits allow students to work up to 20 hours per week during academic sessions and full-time during scheduled breaks.
  • UK: Tier 4 (General) student visa holders can typically work up to 20 hours per week during term time and full-time during vacations.
  • Australia: Student visa holders can work up to 48 hours per fortnight (two weeks) when their course is in session and unlimited hours during scheduled course breaks.
  • New Zealand: Student visa holders can work up to 20 hours per week during the academic year and full-time during scheduled holidays.

Important considerations:

  • Always verify the specific work rights attached to your visa
  • Exceeding permitted work hours can jeopardize your visa status
  • Some courses or institutions may have additional restrictions
  • Work rights may differ for dependents accompanying students

We provide detailed information about work rights specific to your study destination during our consultation process.

How much financial proof do I need for a student visa?

Financial requirements for student visas vary by country and depend on factors such as location, duration of study, and whether dependents are accompanying you. Here are approximate guidelines:

  • USA: You must demonstrate sufficient funds to cover your first year of tuition and living expenses (typically $20,000-$40,000 depending on the institution and location).
  • Canada: You need to show tuition fees for the first year plus CAD $10,000 for living expenses (additional CAD $4,000 for a spouse and CAD $3,000 per child).
  • UK: You must show tuition fees plus living expenses (approximately £1,334 per month for London and £1,023 per month outside London).
  • Australia: You need to demonstrate access to approximately AUD $21,041 per year for living costs, plus tuition fees and travel costs.
  • New Zealand: You need to show NZD $15,000 per year for living expenses, plus tuition fees.

Acceptable forms of financial proof typically include:

  • Bank statements (usually for the past 3-6 months)
  • Education loans
  • Scholarships or grants
  • Sponsorship letters with supporting financial documents
  • Fixed deposits or other investments

We provide detailed guidance on the specific financial requirements and acceptable documentation for your chosen study destination.

Work Visas

Do I need a job offer to apply for a work visa?

Whether you need a job offer depends on the country and the specific work visa category:

  • Job Offer Required:
    • USA: H-1B, L-1, O-1 visas all require a job offer from a US employer
    • UK: Skilled Worker visa requires a job offer from a licensed sponsor
    • Canada: Temporary Work Permit typically requires a job offer with a Labor Market Impact Assessment (LMIA)
    • Australia: Temporary Skill Shortage (TSS) visa requires employer sponsorship
  • No Job Offer Required:
    • Canada: Express Entry (Federal Skilled Worker Program) doesn't require a job offer, though having one adds points
    • Australia: Skilled Independent visa (subclass 189) doesn't require a job offer
    • New Zealand: Skilled Migrant Category can be applied for without a job offer, though having one adds points
    • UK: Global Talent visa and some other specialized categories don't require job offers

Even for visa categories that don't strictly require job offers, having one often:

  • Increases your chances of approval
  • Adds points in points-based immigration systems
  • Expedites processing in some cases

We can help you identify the most suitable work visa category based on your qualifications, experience, and whether you have a job offer.

What is the process for employer sponsorship?

The employer sponsorship process typically involves these key steps:

  1. Employer Eligibility: The employer must be eligible to sponsor foreign workers, which often requires:
    • Registration with immigration authorities
    • Proof of legitimate business operations
    • Demonstration of financial capability
    • Compliance with local labor laws
  2. Labor Market Testing: Many countries require employers to prove they couldn't find a suitable local candidate by:
    • Advertising the position locally for a specified period
    • Documenting recruitment efforts
    • Explaining why local candidates weren't suitable
  3. Sponsorship Application: The employer submits documentation to immigration authorities:
    • Details of the position and why a foreign worker is needed
    • Proof the position meets minimum salary requirements
    • Evidence the role matches the applicant's qualifications
  4. Approval for Sponsorship: The employer receives authorization to sponsor the foreign worker
  5. Worker's Visa Application: Once sponsorship is approved, you (the worker) apply for the actual work visa

Country-specific processes:

  • USA: Employers file Form I-129 for H-1B visas, with an annual quota and lottery system
  • UK: Employers need a Sponsor License and issue a Certificate of Sponsorship to the worker
  • Australia: Employers must be approved sponsors and nominate a position before the worker applies
  • Canada: Employers typically need a Labor Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) before the worker applies for a work permit

We provide guidance to both employers and employees throughout the sponsorship process, ensuring all requirements are met efficiently.

Can my family join me on a work visa?

Most countries allow family members to accompany work visa holders, though specific rules vary:

  • Eligible Family Members: Typically include:
    • Spouse or legal partner
    • Dependent children (usually under 18 or 21, depending on the country)
    • In some cases, dependent parents (though this is less common)
  • Application Process:
    • Family members usually apply for dependent visas
    • Applications can be submitted together with the primary applicant or later
    • Documentation typically includes proof of relationship and financial capability
  • Rights of Dependents: Vary by country:
    • USA: H-4 dependents of H-1B holders can live in the US but have limited work rights (some spouses can apply for work authorization)
    • Canada: Spouses of work permit holders can often apply for open work permits; children can attend public schools
    • UK: Dependents of Skilled Worker visa holders can generally work without restrictions
    • Australia: Dependents of most work visa holders receive full work and study rights
  • Financial Requirements:
    • You must demonstrate sufficient income to support your family
    • Some countries have specific minimum income thresholds that increase with family size

We provide comprehensive guidance on bringing your family with you, including:

  • Advising on the best timing for dependent applications
  • Preparing documentation to prove genuine relationships
  • Explaining work and study rights for dependents
  • Assisting with school enrollment and healthcare access information

Migration & Permanent Residency

What are the most common pathways to permanent residency?

Common pathways to permanent residency include:

  • Skilled Migration:
    • Canada: Express Entry system (Federal Skilled Worker, Federal Skilled Trades, Canadian Experience Class)
    • Australia: General Skilled Migration program (Skilled Independent, Skilled Nominated, Skilled Regional visas)
    • New Zealand: Skilled Migrant Category
  • Employment-Based:
    • USA: EB-1, EB-2, EB-3 visa categories leading to Green Card
    • UK: Skilled Worker visa leading to Indefinite Leave to Remain after 5 years
    • Canada: Provincial Nominee Programs tied to employment
  • Family Sponsorship:
    • Spouse/partner sponsorship
    • Parent sponsorship (in some countries)
    • Child sponsorship
  • Business/Investment:
    • Entrepreneur programs
    • Investor visas
    • Start-up founder visas
  • Study-to-PR Pathways:
    • Canada: Study permit → Post-Graduation Work Permit → Express Entry
    • Australia: Student visa → Post-Study Work visa → Skilled Migration

The most suitable pathway depends on factors such as:

  • Your qualifications and work experience
  • Age (many points-based systems favor younger applicants)
  • Language proficiency
  • Family connections in the destination country
  • Financial resources (especially for investment pathways)
  • Intended destination country and region

We conduct a thorough assessment of your profile to identify the most promising permanent residency pathways and develop a strategic immigration plan tailored to your circumstances.

How long does it take to get permanent residency?

The timeline for obtaining permanent residency varies significantly depending on the country and pathway:

  • Canada:
    • Express Entry: 6-12 months from invitation to apply
    • Provincial Nominee Programs: 12-18 months
    • Family Sponsorship: 12-24 months
  • Australia:
    • Skilled Independent (189): 8-14 months
    • Skilled Nominated (190): 9-15 months
    • Employer Sponsored: 12-18 months
  • USA:
    • Employment-Based Green Cards: 1-5+ years (varies by category and country of birth)
    • Family-Based Green Cards: 1-20+ years (depends on relationship and country of birth)
    • Diversity Visa Lottery: 1-2 years if selected
  • UK:
    • Skilled Worker to Indefinite Leave to Remain: 5 years of residence
    • Spouse visa to settlement: 5 years (or 2-3 years on accelerated routes)
    • Investor/Entrepreneur routes: 2-5 years depending on investment level
  • New Zealand:
    • Skilled Migrant Category: 12-18 months
    • Work to Residence: 2-3 years of work + application processing time

Factors that can affect processing times:

  • Application volume and backlogs
  • Completeness and accuracy of your application
  • Verification processes for education and work experience
  • Security and medical checks
  • Country-specific quotas and caps
  • Policy changes and administrative delays

We provide realistic timeline estimates based on current processing times and help you develop a strategic plan that considers these timeframes.

What is a points-based immigration system?

A points-based immigration system evaluates candidates based on objective criteria, assigning points for various factors to determine eligibility. Countries like Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and the UK use points-based systems for skilled migration.

Common factors that earn points include:

  • Age: Most systems favor applicants in their 25-35 age range
  • Education: Higher qualifications generally earn more points
  • Work Experience: Relevant skilled work experience, with more points for longer experience
  • Language Proficiency: Higher scores in language tests (IELTS, CELPIP, etc.) earn more points
  • Adaptability: Previous study or work in the country, spouse qualifications, relatives in the country
  • Job Offer: Having a valid job offer often adds significant points
  • State/Provincial Nomination: Nomination by a state or province can add substantial points

How points-based systems work:

  • Each country sets a minimum points threshold for eligibility
  • Candidates who meet or exceed the threshold enter a pool of eligible applicants
  • Regular draws select the highest-ranking candidates from the pool
  • Selected candidates receive invitations to apply for permanent residency

Examples of points-based systems:

  • Canada's Express Entry: Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) with scores up to 1,200 points
  • Australia's SkillSelect: Points test with a minimum threshold of 65 points
  • New Zealand's Skilled Migrant Category: Points system with a minimum of 160 points
  • UK's Points-Based Immigration System: Different pathways with specific points requirements

We provide detailed points assessments to determine your eligibility and develop strategies to maximize your points score, such as improving language test scores, gaining additional qualifications, or exploring provincial/state nomination options.
